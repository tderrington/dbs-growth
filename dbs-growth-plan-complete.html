Guiding principle. No phase is initiated on projected revenue — only on work in hand or under contract. Each hire should pay for itself before the next one is needed. Growth is a consequence of the work, not a goal unto itself.

The plan in one sentence. Every phase removes roles from people who are overloaded and gives them to people whose full-time job it is to do that work well. The principal goes from seven roles to two — and eventually to optional. The original team members evolve from generalists into leaders. Nobody is asked to do less — everyone is asked to go deeper.

Phases 0–4 build a complete, self-sustaining 13-person studio. At Phase 4, the road forks: stop and run a lifestyle studio, or continue to 21 for a transferable practice that can be sold to an external buyer. Click any phase above or use the tabs to navigate.

The Decision Point

Phase 4 is where the road forks. Everything before this is identical regardless of which path you choose. The question is what you build on top of it — and the answer depends entirely on whether you intend to exit the practice.

Path A — Lifestyle Studio
Stop at 13
A well-run design studio that funds a good life. The principal stays involved in design leadership and client relationships indefinitely. Revenue: $2.9–3.3M. Exit is internal — a managed wind-down, or a below-market sale to loyal senior staff.
Nothing wrong with this. Most architecture firms take this path.
or
Path B — Transferable Practice
Continue to 21
A multi-leader firm with operational maturity to survive a change in ownership. Two Associate Principals share client relationships and design authority. Real EBITDA attracts external buyers — a larger firm, a regional consolidator, a PE-backed platform.
More complexity. But the firm becomes an asset that can be sold, not just a job that can be quit.

Why 13 is dangerous for an exit. At 13 people the firm is too large to run without real overhead but too small to spread that overhead across enough revenue-generating staff for meaningful profit margin. Key-person dependency is very high. EBITDA is $330–500K at best. Likely sale value: $1.0–2.0M. The buyer pool is essentially internal only.

Why 21 changes the math. At 21, two equity-holding principals hold independent client relationships. EBITDA reaches $770K–1.17M. With DBS's niche premium in experiential recreation, enterprise value reaches $3.1–6.4M. The buyer pool opens to consolidators, PE platforms, and larger firms looking to acquire a market position.

The decision to pursue the exit path should be made at or before Phase 4, even if the actual sale is a decade away. The structural changes in Phases 5 and 6 — equity sharing, leadership development, BD institutionalization — each take 2–3 years to mature.

06 Phase 6 — Transferable Practice

21 FTE + contracts · 5–7 years out · Revenue trigger: $5.5–6.5M

The firm is exit-ready. Two Principals hold 30–40% combined equity and can run the firm without the founder. The Managing Principal owns schedule, budget, and staffing; the Design Principal owns design quality, interiors, and external reputation. When those domains conflict — a project behind schedule with unresolved design — the Managing Principal makes the call on timeline, the Design Principal makes the call on what ships. If they cannot resolve it, the Founding Principal (while active) or the governance agreement breaks the tie.

The BD function generates opportunities independently through the BD & Marketing Manager, with strategic relationships transitioning from the founder to the Principals over a 2–3 year runway before exit. Financial reporting is clean, EBITDA is consistent, and no single client represents more than 15–20% of revenue.

The niche premium. DBS's specialization in experiential venues, climbing centers, and hospitality is the single most valuable thing for exit purposes. Firms with a definable niche command premium multiples because acquirers are buying a market position, not just a book of business. A generalist firm at 21 might sell for 3.5x. A firm that owns "experiential recreation architecture in the Sun Belt" could sell for 5x or more.

Exit Readiness Conditions

Leadership depth. Two equity-holding principals who hold independent client relationships, make decisions without the founder, and are contractually committed to staying 3–5 years post-sale.

Revenue quality. No single client exceeds 15–20% of NSR. Pipeline managed through CRM with documented BD processes. Backlog documentation is clean. Revenue recognition follows consistent percentage-of-completion methodology.

Operational maturity. Financial reporting is GAAP-compliant and buyer-grade. Employment records, contracts, insurance, and licenses are current. The Operations Director has prepared the firm for due diligence. Profitability consistent across 3+ years.

Niche defensibility. The firm owns a definable market position — experiential recreation architecture — that a buyer cannot easily replicate through organic hiring. The brand, portfolio, and client relationships represent a moat that justifies a premium multiple.

The most common mistake principals make with exit planning is starting too late. The structural changes in Phases 5 and 6 — equity sharing, leadership development, BD institutionalization, financial cleanup — each take 2–3 years to mature. A 20-year exit horizon gives you room. A 10-year horizon does not.
